Platform vs. Product Thinking

Products serve users. Platforms serve ecosystems.

A product solves a problem for one type of user. A platform creates value by connecting multiple groups — creators and consumers, buyers and sellers, advertisers and audiences. The strategic decisions are fundamentally different: a product PM asks "what features do my users need?" A platform PM asks "how do I create rules and incentives so that participants create value for each other?" Platform decisions are about designing systems, not building features. Every choice affects multiple stakeholder groups, often with competing interests.

How to apply it

For every platform decision, map all affected stakeholder groups and their incentives. A change that helps creators (more distribution) might hurt consumers (lower content quality). A change that helps advertisers (more ad inventory) might hurt the user experience. The platform PM's job is to find the equilibrium where all sides get enough value to keep participating. When trade-offs are unavoidable, prioritise the side that's harder to replace — usually the supply side (creators, sellers) in early stages, and the demand side (consumers, buyers) once supply is abundant.

Example

Pinterest's shift from "social network" to "visual discovery platform" was a platform thinking decision. As a social network, Pinterest competed with Facebook and Instagram for social interaction. As a visual discovery platform, Pinterest competed with Google for commercial intent. The strategic pivot changed everything: the feed algorithm shifted from social signals (what friends pinned) to interest signals (what YOU would find useful). This attracted advertisers because Pinterest users were actively searching for things to buy — not scrolling past friends' vacation photos.

Platform Decision Framework

  1. Stakeholder Map — Who are all the groups on your platform? (Creators, consumers, advertisers, developers, merchants.) List them all.
  2. Value Exchange — What value does each group give to the platform? What value does each group receive? Map the exchanges.
  3. Incentive Alignment — Where are incentives aligned across groups? Where do they conflict? Conflicts need explicit trade-off decisions.
  4. Supply vs. Demand — Which side is harder to replace right now? Prioritise that side. The answer changes as the platform matures.
  5. Rules & Governance — What rules does the platform set? (Content policies, revenue sharing, quality standards.) Rules shape behaviour.
  6. Network Effects — Does adding one more user make the platform more valuable for everyone? If yes, invest in growth. If no, invest in quality.
  7. Platform Risk — What happens if one group leaves? (Multi-homing risk, disintermediation, platform dependence.) Identify and mitigate.

Strategic Trade-offs & Positioning

Strategy is choosing what NOT to do

Michael Porter's fundamental insight: strategy isn't about being the best — it's about being different. Every platform must choose its position: broad or focused, premium or mass-market, content-first or commerce-first. These choices create trade-offs that competitors can't easily copy. A platform that tries to be everything to everyone ends up being nothing to anyone. The hardest strategic decisions are the ones where both options are good — choosing one means sacrificing the other.

How to apply it

Define your platform's strategic position in one sentence: "We are the [specific thing] for [specific audience]." If you can't complete this sentence clearly, your strategy isn't clear. Then test every product decision against this position: does this feature reinforce our position or dilute it? Features that appeal to everyone but differentiate you from no one are strategically worthless. The best platforms have clear "we don't do that" boundaries that feel counterintuitive but reinforce their moat.

Example

Pinterest's decision NOT to add direct messaging, stories, or social feeds — despite every competitor having them — was a strategic positioning decision. Pinterest's position: "We are the visual discovery engine for planning and shopping." Adding social features would make Pinterest more like Instagram (where it would lose), not more like itself. The "no social" decision felt risky when Instagram was growing 10x faster, but it preserved Pinterest's unique position as the place people go with commercial intent — which is far more valuable per user to advertisers.

Strategic Positioning Template

  1. Position Statement — "We are the [what] for [whom]." One sentence. If it takes a paragraph, it's not a strategy.
  2. Differentiators — What do we do that competitors can't easily copy? (Unique data, unique behaviour, unique network.)
  3. Trade-offs — What do we explicitly NOT do? What features have we rejected? These define our boundaries.
  4. Competitive Set — Who do we compete with for user attention and money? Who do we NOT compete with? Be specific.
  5. Value Chain Position — Where do we sit between content creation and consumption? Between discovery and purchase?
  6. Moat Assessment — How defensible is our position? (Network effects, data moats, switching costs, brand.) Rate each 1-5.
  7. Strategic Coherence — Does every major product decision reinforce the position? List the last 5 decisions and check alignment.

Multi-Sided Platform Dynamics

The chicken-and-egg problem — and how to solve it

Multi-sided platforms face a fundamental cold-start problem: creators won't come without an audience, and audiences won't come without content. Advertisers won't come without users, and users won't stay without a good (ad-funded) experience. Solving this chicken-and-egg problem requires sequencing: which side do you attract first, and how do you create enough value for that side before the other side arrives? The answer usually involves subsidising one side or single-player mode — making the product useful even without the other side.

How to apply it

Identify which side of your platform can get value independently (single-player mode). Build for that side first. Pinterest solved the cold-start problem by making the product useful as a personal bookmarking tool — you could save and organise images even if no one else was on the platform. This attracted users who created content (pins) as a byproduct of their personal use. The content then attracted more users through search and social sharing. The virtuous cycle started from a single-player use case.

Example

Pinterest's early growth strategy was to solve the cold-start problem by seeding content from the open web. The team built browser extensions and "Pin It" buttons that let early users save images from any website. This meant Pinterest had millions of high-quality images before it had millions of users. New users who arrived found a visually rich, useful product on day one — not an empty platform waiting for content. The content seeding strategy meant Pinterest never faced the "empty room" problem that killed many social networks.

Cold-Start Strategy Playbook

  1. Single-Player Value — Can one side use the platform alone? (Pinterest: personal bookmarking. Yelp: reading reviews.) Build for this first.
  2. Content Seeding — Can you populate the platform with content before users arrive? (Aggregation, partnerships, user-generated from other uses.)
  3. Subsidy Strategy — Which side do you subsidise to get started? (Free for creators, paid by advertisers. Or free for consumers, paid by merchants.)
  4. Sequencing — Which side first? Usually: content/supply side first → attracts demand → demand attracts more supply → flywheel starts.
  5. Atomic Network — What's the minimum viable network? (One city? One niche? One use case?) Start small and dense, not broad and thin.
  6. Cross-Side Effects — How does growth on one side affect the other? (More pins → better discovery → more users → more pins.)
  7. Tipping Point — At what point does the network become self-sustaining? Define this threshold and measure progress toward it.

Platform Monetisation Strategy

Who pays, when, and how — without killing the ecosystem

Platform monetisation is a strategic decision, not just a revenue question. Who you charge affects who participates. How much you charge affects how much value remains for the ecosystem. When you introduce monetisation affects whether participants feel the platform is "for them" or "exploiting them." The most successful platforms monetise by inserting themselves into the value chain — taking a cut when value is exchanged — rather than extracting fees from participants for access.

How to apply it

Map the value exchanges on your platform. Find the moment when the most value is created or exchanged — that's where monetisation has the least friction. Pinterest monetises at the moment of commercial intent (promoted pins) because that's when advertisers capture the most value. The user sees a relevant product; the advertiser gets a qualified lead; Pinterest takes a fee. Everyone wins because the monetisation is aligned with the value creation moment, not extracted from it.

Example

Pinterest's monetisation journey illustrates strategic patience. For years, Pinterest resisted ads despite investor pressure, because premature monetisation would have killed the user experience before the platform reached critical mass. When they did introduce Promoted Pins in 2014, they made ads look and behave exactly like organic pins — searchable, saveable, and visually native. This decision traded short-term ad revenue (flashy banner ads generate more clicks) for long-term platform health (native ads don't drive users away). By 2023, Pinterest's ad revenue per user was growing faster than Meta's despite having 1/6th the user base.

Platform Monetisation Framework

  1. Value Creation Map — Where is the most value created on the platform? (Discovery, transaction, engagement, data.) Monetise near value creation.
  2. Who Pays? — The side that captures the most value should pay. (Advertisers pay because they capture commercial intent. Users pay when they get premium value.)
  3. Monetisation Timing — Too early kills growth. Too late leaves money on the table. Monetise when the platform is self-sustaining, not before.
  4. Native vs. Extractive — Does the monetisation feel like part of the experience (promoted pins) or an interruption (pop-up ads)? Native always wins long-term.
  5. Take Rate — What percentage of value do you capture? Too high (>30%) and participants leave. Too low (<5%) and the business isn't viable.
  6. Ecosystem Health — Monitor whether monetisation is growing the pie (more value for everyone) or shrinking it (extracting from participants).
  7. Revenue Diversification — One revenue stream is fragile. Build 2-3 monetisation models that reinforce each other (ads + commerce + subscriptions).
